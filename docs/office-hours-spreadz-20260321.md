## Problem Statement

Spreadz is trying to become a mobile-first, real-time group chat app for Indian college students, centered on swipeable live rooms, Hinglish conversation, and real identities.

The stronger version of the thesis is not merely "students want chat." It is: students have long wanted large, lively community spaces, but those products historically collapse into spam, abuse, or low-trust chaos because moderation was too weak or too expensive. AI moderation may now make a previously unstable category viable.

The immediate question is not "can this be built?" but "is this worth pushing hard right now while still pre-launch and at 0 users?"

## Demand Evidence

Current hard evidence is weak, but the enabling-technology argument is stronger than a generic "maybe people want this."

- There are no active users yet.
- There is no observed behavior showing students would be upset if Spreadz disappeared tomorrow.
- There is no proof yet that students will switch away from their existing campus communication habits.
- The strongest signal so far is founder conviction plus a fairly complete prototype.

There is, however, a plausible category-level insight:

- people repeatedly gravitate toward large community chat products when those products feel alive
- many of those spaces degrade when trust and moderation break
- AI may lower the cost of keeping those spaces usable

That is enough to justify a focused experiment, but not enough to justify a broad all-in push.

## Status Quo

Assumption based on the product category and common student behavior: Indian college students already coordinate and socialize through a mix of WhatsApp class groups, hostel groups, Instagram DMs and confession pages, Telegram channels, Discord servers, and in-person campus life.

That workaround has real gaps:

- Conversations are fragmented by batch, branch, hostel, or friend group.
- Discovery across campus is weak.
- WhatsApp exposes phone numbers.
- Anonymous pages create low trust and moderation problems.
- Discord and Telegram communities are often static and utility-first; they do not create the feeling of an alive, algorithmically explorable campus public square.

But the status quo is still strong because it is already dense, already installed, and already where students' friends are.

The additional nuance is that public or semi-public social spaces often fail not because no one wants them, but because they become unreadable, unsafe, or embarrassing to participate in once moderation falls behind.

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge

The most promising early user is not "all Indian college students."

It is:

- a student at one specific college
- who wants to know what people outside their immediate clique are talking about right now
- during moments when campus attention converges naturally
- who gets value from seeing campus mood in public, not only from joining a private study or friend group
- who feels shy approaching classmates or campus acquaintances offline, but might talk inside a smaller room without the pressure of a visible friend audience

The narrowest wedge worth testing is:

One campus, one dense time window, and 3-5 high-salience rooms around things students already care about this week:

- fest drama
- placements and internships
- exams and faculty gossip
- freshers/juniors/seniors social dynamics

If Spreadz cannot feel alive in that tiny wedge, it will not work as a broad campus social network.

The additional product claim from the founder is that topic-routing and room-swiping matter: students do not just want a server full of channels; they want a feed-like system that keeps showing them the next interesting campus conversation.

The founder also introduced a social-proof insight: the first feeling does not have to be "my exact college is already packed." It can be "students like me nearby are active, and people I know may already be here." That may be enough to reduce posting hesitation in the first sessions.

The founder added another wedge too: Spreadz may help students talk to people they already know of, or see around campus, but would not approach offline because college social dynamics are large, awkward, and high-friction.

## Constraints

- Cold start is brutal for consumer chat. Empty rooms kill habit fast.
- "Real identities" are the product thesis, but the current implementation still uses anonymous auth and optional college entry.
- Real identity may improve trust, but it may also suppress the exact spicy posting behavior that makes campus apps fun.
- AI moderation may be the enabling shift, but only if it is good enough that students notice the space feels better, and lightweight enough that conversation still feels alive.
- Moderation is not optional in Indian college communities; harassment and ragging dynamics are real.
- A swipe-based room experience only works if there is always another room worth swiping into.
- The algorithm must consistently surface interesting rooms; if it feels random or dead, the product collapses into a worse Discord.
- High-conflict prompts can create instant activity, but the wrong type of controversy can destroy safety, trust, and female participation.
- Multi-campus expansion before one-campus density would likely spread the product too thin.

## Premises

1. People do want lively community spaces, and many historical failures were at least partly moderation failures. - Plausible and worth taking seriously
2. The algorithmic room/feed model creates materially more engagement than static Discord or Telegram channels. - Core product premise
3. AI moderation can keep a campus chat legible and safe without making it feel sterile or over-policed. - Core unproven premise
4. Small-group rooms can lower social hesitation enough that students talk to people they would not approach offline. - Strong product premise, still unproven
5. Real identity will raise quality more than it lowers posting volume. - High-risk premise
6. One college can reach enough density fast enough to make room-swiping feel exciting. - Plausible, not yet proven
7. Verification must become real if it is part of the pitch. - Strongly agree

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: Single-Campus Moderated Public Square

Summary:
Focus Spreadz on one college, one launch window, and a handful of operator-seeded live rooms, but make the explicit wedge "the campus public square that does not go feral." Measure whether better moderation and trust make students participate more, not less, and whether swiping between active topics feels better than navigating static channels.

Effort: M
Risk: Medium

Pros:

- Fastest way to learn whether the core behavior exists
- Maximizes density where density matters
- Lets you test the swipe-room thesis without building a full social graph first
- Directly tests the user's claim that moderation is the missing unlock
- Directly tests the user's claim that algorithmic topic discovery beats static servers

Cons:

- Feels much smaller than the grand vision
- Requires strong campus-level distribution and manual seeding
- Results may look bad if distribution is weak even when the concept has some merit
- Moderation quality becomes part of the core product, not just back-office ops

Reuses:

- Current realtime room chat
- Swipe-first room navigation
- Existing room/message analytics foundation

### Approach B: Verified Campus Identity Network

Summary:
Double down on the "real identities" thesis: real verification, persistent profiles, friend graph, moderation tooling, and a strong trust layer before trying to scale.

Effort: XL
Risk: High

Pros:

- Best long-term moat if it works
- Clearer differentiation from anonymous gossip pages
- Could build durable trust and safer interactions

Cons:

- More friction before users experience any magic
- Harder cold start because identity without density is boring
- Slower learning loop at the exact stage when fast learning matters most

Reuses:

- Existing profile, avatar, friend, and reporting concepts

### Approach C: Distribution-First Campus Operator Layer

Summary:
Treat Spreadz less like a pure app and more like a campus pulse machine. Seed content and traffic through ambassadors, Instagram pages, and WhatsApp communities, and use the app as the live interaction destination.

Effort: M
Risk: Medium

Pros:

- Better fit for how student communities already form
- Gives you a way to manufacture density early
- Lets the product borrow trust and reach from existing campus nodes

Cons:

- Less "pure product" and more operations-heavy
- Harder to scale if the behavior never becomes self-sustaining
- Can hide weak product-market pull if operators do too much

Reuses:

- Current room-based interaction model
- Headline-led room discovery

### Approach D: Weak-Tie Social Bridge

Summary:
Position Spreadz as the safest way to talk to college people you know of but would never DM or approach directly. Use small rooms and shared topics to reduce social risk while preserving real identity.

Effort: M
Risk: Medium

Pros:

- Targets a real emotional pain point: hesitation
- Gives students a reason to participate beyond pure entertainment
- Could create uniquely sticky "I finally talked to them" moments

Cons:

- Requires careful room design so it feels safe rather than exposing
- May conflict with highly public, high-heat conversation dynamics
- Harder to message clearly if the product is trying to be both public square and soft social bridge

Reuses:

- Current room mechanics
- Identity layer
- Swipe-based discovery

## Recommended Approach

Choose Approach A, supported by some tactics from Approach C.

Do not push hard on the broad vision yet. Push hard on a one-campus falsification test.

The revised goal is not just to prove one campus becomes more alive because Spreadz exists. It is to prove that one campus becomes more alive because Spreadz is both lively and well-moderated. If your core insight is right, users should feel that difference.

The test should also check whether Spreadz reduces social hesitation. If students begin talking to classmates or acquaintances they would not have approached offline, that is an additional sign of real value.

In this session, the founder chose the primary week-one priority clearly: public posting and message volume matter more than weak-tie social bridging at first. The north star is messages sent, because high message velocity implies rooms feel alive and students find the product worth returning to.

## Open Questions

- Which specific college can you dominate first?
- Do you personally have distribution into that campus, or will you need ambassadors?
- Do you have any trusted human distribution at all, or only cold access like student email lists?
- Is "real identity" non-negotiable, or are you willing to loosen it if posting volume dies?
- What exactly should AI moderation do in-product: block, downrank, summarize, warn, slow, or escalate to humans?
- What are the top 3 room themes that reliably create same-night participation?
- Are students actually craving cross-clique discovery, or are they mostly satisfied inside existing group chats?
- What does the algorithm do in the first 5 minutes for a new user so the product feels more alive than Discord?
- How will seeded content be useful and resonant without pretending to be real student conversation?
- How quickly does the experience move from "nearby students are active" to "my people are here too"?
- Which matters more in week one: campus-wide public energy or lower-pressure small-room interaction with weak ties?

## Seeding Insight

The founder surfaced an important early-growth mechanic: empty rooms kill the product, and naive AI-generated fake conversation will likely be detected and rejected.

The stronger seeding strategy is not "fake users talking."

It is:

- operator-seeded spark content
- adapted from real internet discourse patterns rather than generic AI filler
- rewritten into campus-relevant language and Hinglish tone
- localized to actual student concerns, slangs, and live moments
- used to start rooms and prompts, not to impersonate existing student consensus
- designed to say something strong enough that a real student feels compelled to reply

This distinction matters. If the app makes users think conversation is already happening when it is actually synthetic, trust may collapse. If the app instead gives students a highly resonant prompt, summary, or starter take that invites real replies, that can be a legitimate seed.

## Magic Moment

The founder identified a plausible retention mechanic:

- a student sees a sharp public prompt
- says something they genuinely believe
- other students respond quickly
- the student feels heard, seen, or backed up in public

That is a real "come back tomorrow" loop.

There may also be a second magic moment:

- a student talks naturally with someone they already know of, or see around campus
- the room format lowers hesitation
- the interaction feels easier than real life

That is a different but also powerful retention loop.

But the product should distinguish between:

- high-energy prompts that create expression and belonging
- destructive prompts that create pile-ons, identity conflict, or safety collapse

Low-to-medium stakes conflict may be valuable:

- exam panic honesty
- placement flex versus reality
- campus canteen or hostel debates
- branch rivalries
- taste wars around music, anime, movies, or sports

High-risk conflict is likely too dangerous as a primary growth loop:

- religion
- caste
- communal politics
- targeted attacks on identifiable students

## Success Criteria

Treat this as a go/no-go test, not vanity metrics.

- 75-100 invited students from one campus in one launch window
- 25+ students posting at least once in the first 7 days
- At least 3 rooms that feel active without founder prompting
- 30-40% day-1 retention among activated users
- 15-20% day-7 retention among activated users
- At least 5 users who spontaneously ask where the app is when activity slows down
- Evidence that at least some users experienced the "I posted and people actually listened" magic moment
- Evidence that at least some users talked to someone they normally would not have approached offline
- Messages sent is the primary north-star metric for the first launch

If you miss badly on these after a serious, concentrated campus launch, the answer is probably "not worth pushing hard in its current form."

## Dependencies

- A real first-campus distribution plan
- Better truth in the identity promise
- A concrete AI moderation policy and failure-handling plan
- Room seeding plan for the first week
- Clear moderation and reporting policy
- Tight instrumentation on activation, posting, and return behavior
- A clear editorial policy for seeded prompts versus real user conversation
- At least one real distribution owner who can get students into the app at the same time

## The Assignment

Run a 7-day single-campus launch test.

Before that, separate two experiments:

- smoke test: can 10-15 recruited friends create enough message velocity to validate room seeding, prompt quality, and moderation flow?
- real demand test: can actual target students from one campus be activated at the same time without hand-holding?

For the smoke test, the key variable is overlap, not total signups. A useful target is:

- 20-30 people invited
- 12-20 people actually online in the same 15-20 minute window
- 30-45 minutes total session time requested from participants

People do not all need to stay for hours. What matters is one concentrated burst where rooms feel alive.

Before launch:

- pick one campus
- recruit 3-5 student operators or ambassadors
- define 5 launch rooms tied to things students already care about this week
- tighten the identity story so it is truthful
- define the seeding format: prompt card, summary card, operator post, or real ambassador post
- make sure seeded content is useful and local without pretending to be organic student chatter

During launch:

- personally watch students use it live
- note where they stall, lurk, or bounce
- measure whether anyone comes back without being chased

Suggested smoke-test format:

- ask people to reserve 45 minutes
- make the first 20 minutes the heavy overlap window
- for a 12-20 person test, do not split everyone across 5 live rooms immediately
- start with 1 main live room and 2 optional side rooms at most
- use the rest of the "seeded" content as prompt cards or starter posts, not fake human conversations
- tell every participant to send at least 3 messages in the first 10 minutes
- watch whether conversation becomes self-sustaining after the initial push

Minimum useful pass signal:

- 12+ people overlap live
- 3 rooms get active
- 60-100 total messages get sent
- at least some messages are responses to each other, not only replies to seeded prompts

Recommended first-session room shape:

- Room 1: the main room where everyone starts together
- Room 2: a second room opened only if the main room gets enough energy
- Room 3: optional niche/fun room if message velocity is already strong

For this size test, prompt cards are safer than fake seeded discussions. They can be strong, controversial, local, and Hinglish, but they should read like conversation starters rather than disguised student traffic.

After 7 days:

- if one campus starts to feel alive, keep pushing
- if rooms stay empty or only move when you force them, do not brute-force the current thesis
